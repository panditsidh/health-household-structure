\section{Introduction}

Family relationships play a central role in shaping health and well being. Demographers have investigated the relationships between family structure and relationships among household members, on the one hand, and access to resources, social support, and health outcomes, on the other \citep{lindemann2019family, waite2000family, umberson2010social}. For example, marriage has been found to benefit physical health, psychological well being, and even survival in many contexts \citep{ross1990impact, begashaw2016health, lewis2004conceptualization, soulsby2015marriage, robles2014marital}. Further, research have found that marital quality impacts maternal health through the mechanisms of timely and adequate antenatal care, improved emotional well being during and after pregnancy, and reducing high risk behavior like drinking and smoking during pregnancy (\cite{alipour2019relationship,rosand2011partner}).

India, home to one in six of the world’s births, provides an important context to examine how family relationships impact maternal health.  In India, pregnancy occurs almost exclusively within the context of marriages that have been arranged by family members (\citep{banerji2021does} .  Husbands and wives may not meet one another before their weddings \citep{desai2010gender}.  There is, however, substantial variation in the household structures in which pregnant women reside.  Married women may live in nuclear households, consisting of only the couple and their children, or in patrilocal extended family households, also called ``joint households.''  Joint households are those in which a woman resides with her husband’s parents, siblings, and other relatives. Although patrilocal joint residence has historically been the normative family structure in India, it is not substantially more prevalent than nuclear residence \citep{breton2021modernisation}.  A minority of women live with in their natal homes, that is, with their own parents, siblings, or relatives.  Their husbands may or may not reside with them.

Household structure may shape women’s well-being through multiple mechanisms. Patrilocal joint households are often characterized by pooled economic resources, intergenerational support, and shared domestic labor \citep{wadley1988karimpur}.  At the same time, hierarchies based on gender and generation may mean that senior family members, particularly mothers-in-law, exercise control over younger women’s mobility and decision making \citep{desai2005women, bloom2001dimensions}. \cite{gupta1999lifeboat} identifies newly married women as the  vulnerable members of the household who are required to demonstrate deference and submission to other family members, and to bear the largest burden of housework. Prior research demonstrates how hierarchies within joint households impact women’s health and that of their children. \cite{coffey2022mothers} find that women married to a younger brother have worse maternal health than women married to an older brother in the same household, and that their children have lower chances of survival and poorer growth.

Nuclear households, in contrast, may provide greater scope for spousal communication and individual autonomy, but may lack the economic and caregiving support available in extended families. Married women in nuclear households are less likely to face the same pressure from their in-laws. The absence of a senior kin may increase women’s participation in household decision making \citep{simkhada2010role,char2010influence}. Further, when husbands in nuclear households migrate for work, women become the head of the household, assuming primary responsibility for  household management, further enhancing her decision making authority \citep{desai2008negotiated}. 

This paper investigates how household structure shapes pregnant women's autonomy and their use of maternal health care.  Our findings cohere with characterizations of nuclear and joint households in the literature: in all of the three survey rounds that we analyze -- from 2005, 2014, and 2021 -- pregnant women in nuclear households have greater decision-making power relative to pregnant women in joint households, but are less likely to use of maternal health care.  We also ask whether and how these relationships have changed in the past two decades.  Differences by household type have narrowed, which is consistent with rapid improvement in the accessibility of maternal health care during this period, as well as with important increases in women's decision-making power across household types.\citep{ram2026investigation, gandhi2022predictors, muralidharan2017cycling, rai2022use} 

This paper builds upon and dialog with prior empirical research by \cite{allendorf2013going}, who uses 1992 to 2005 data from India to show that several women's health outcomes were positively associated with joint, rather than nuclear family, residence, during that period.  \cite{allendorf2013going}'s paper noted an approximately 8 percentage point increase in the proportion of young women living in nuclear households between 1992 and 2005, and raised the question of how, considering the joint family health advantages that she documented, the shift towards nuclear families might impact women's health in the future.  In contrast, our research, which specifically focuses on pregnant women, finds that in the last two decades, the trend towards nuclear households has reversed.  Pregnant women are actually 6 percentage points less likely to be living in nuclear households in 2021 than they were in 2005. However, one thing that remains consistent with the period \cite{allendorf2013going} studied is that joint families are still wealthier than nuclear families, and wealth is a mediator that explains part of the observed gap between patrilocal and nuclear households in facility births and 4+ ANC visits. Consistent with this, the inclusion of wealth controls substantially reduced the magnitude of these differences but does not eliminate them entirely.

Among this paper's substantive contributions to the literature is documenting the reversal in the trend towards nuclear household structure among pregnant women in India. Along with consistently showing that pregnant women in joint households have lower decision making power than their counterparts in nuclear households the same doesn't seem to happening in case of maternal healthcare utilization. Thus, indicating lower decision making power does not translate into lower maternal healthcare utilization rather, it operates through a different mechanism which is largely explained by economic resources. 



\section{Data and methodology}
\subsection{Data}

We use data from India’s National Family Health Surveys (NFHS) rounds 3 (2005–06), 4 (2015–16), and 5 (2019–21). The NFHS is India’s Demographic and Health Survey, a nationally representative cross-sectional survey of households and women aged 15–49.

The sample for our primary analysis are those women who are ever married, currently pregnant\footnote{Appendix Table \ref{...} shows that women who report being one or two months pregnant differ systematically from those reporting three or more months of pregnancy. In particular, early pregnancy is more likely to be recognized or reported among more educated and wealthier women. We therefore exclude women reporting one or two months of pregnancy from the analysis.}, and living in either a nuclear or patrilocal household structure. We exclude women living in natal households (about 15 percent of the sample), as this living arrangement represents a substantively different social context from both patrilocal and nuclear households. The prevalence of natal residence is stable across survey rounds, so excluding this group does not affect the interpretation of trends.  We use this sample to analyze trends in household structure, and their relationship with autonomy outcomes. For analyses of maternal health care utilization, instead of focusing on pregnant women, we restrict the sample to ever married women who have given birth in the 3-12 months before the survey. 

\subsection{Variables}

\subsubsection{Household structure}
We construct the household structure variable using information on the members residing in the same household as the pregnant woman. We define nuclear households as ones where the woman and her husband are the only two adult members in the household. Patrilocal joint households are defined as the ones where the woman lives with her husband’s adult kin that is if the household has members such as parent-in-law and sibling-in-law. Natal households are those in which the pregnant woman is the daughter or the sister of the household head and none of her husband’s adult kin are present. 

\subsubsection{Outcome variables} \label{sec:outcomes}
To understand women's autonomy during pregnancy we construct outcomes based on two questions: who usually makes decisions about healthcare for yourself?  And, who usually makes decisions about visits to your family or relatives? We dichotomize these measures by coding women who report that their husband or someone else makes the decision on their behalf as ‘no say,’ and those who report deciding jointly with their husband or being the only decision maker as ‘some say’. To measure maternal healthcare utilization among recently postpartum women, we examine two indicators: birth in a health facility (as opposed to at home) and having 4 or more antenatal care (ANC) visits. The four visit threshold follows the World Health Organization (WHO) and Government of India’s National Health Mission guidelines which suggested this number of visits until 2016.\footnote{In 2016 WHO and NRHM increased the recommended minimum number of visits to 8.  We use 4 because the median pregnant woman in our data was pregnant in 2015.}

To capture differences in wealth across household types, we control for household ownership of a set of asset indicators. These include indicators for whether the household has finished floors, electricity, a radio, a television, a refrigerator, a bicycle, a car, and a latrine or toilet. All variables are coded as binary indicators.

\subsection{Analytical Strategy}
\subsubsection{Regression Models}

We estimate linear probability models (LPMs) to examine the association between household structure and the four binary outcomes: (1) reporting ``no say'' in decisions about the respondent's own healthcare, (2) reporting ``no say'' in decisions about visits to family or relatives, (3) having an institutional delivery (birth in a health facility), and (4) receiving at least four antenatal care (ANC) visits.\footnote{All outcomes are coded as binary indicators.}

For woman $i$ in state $s$, our baseline specification is:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:nocontrols}
Y_{is} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{Patrilocal}_{is} + \gamma_s + \epsilon_{is},
\end{equation}

where $i$ indexes women and $s$ indexes states. $Y_{is}$ denotes the outcomes described above, $\text{Patrilocal}_{is}$ is an indicator for residing in a patrilocal joint household (with nuclear households as the reference category), $\gamma_s$ are state fixed effects, and $\epsilon_{is}$ is an error term. We estimate this equation separately for each NFHS round: NFHS-3 (2005--06), NFHS-4 (2015--16), and NFHS-5 (2019--21) to examine how the relationships between household structure and the outcomes change over time.

To assess the role of household economic status, we estimate an additional specification that includes wealth:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:controls}
Y_{is} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{Patrilocal}_{is} + \alpha W_{is} + \gamma_s + \epsilon_{is}.
\end{equation}

In all models, we report standard errors clustered at the primary sampling unit level to account for within-cluster correlation of outcomes. $W_{is}$ is a vector of wealth variables described above, and $\alpha$ is the corresponding vector of coefficients.\footnote{All models additionally account for the NFHS complex survey design using sampling weights and stratification.}


\section{Results}
\subsection{Trends in Patrilocal Residence}

Table \ref{tab:proppatrilocal} shows that the share of pregnant women living in patrilocal households have increased from 55 percent in 2005-2006 to 62 percent in 2019-2021. This increase is observed across all major social groups, with the largest increase among OBC women (11 percentage points) and smaller increases among Adivasi and Muslim women (2 percentage points each)\footnote{Social groups are defined by combining caste and religion. Women are classified as Adivasi or Dalit if they report belonging to a Scheduled Tribe or Scheduled Caste, respectively. Muslim women who are not SC/ST are classified as Muslim. Hindu or Sikh women belonging to Other Backward Classes are classified as OBC. Hindu women who are not SC, ST, or OBC are classified as Forward caste. Women who do not fall into these categories (e.g., Christian, Jain, Sikh outside OBC, or no religion) are excluded (2.3 percent in NFHS-5).}.  Across regions, patrilocal residence increases in all regions except the East. High levels of patrilocal residence were observed particularly in the North and West by NFHS 5 which were 72 and 67 percent respectively. Even areas with comparatively lower baseline levels of patrilocal residence such as Northeast and South showed a noticeable increase in patrilocal residence.  

Patrilocal residence varies sharply by age and parity. In 2019-21, over 80 percent of pregnant women aged 15-19 reside in patrilocal households, compared to about 30 percent among women aged 40-44. First-time mothers (parity 0) are more likely to reside in patrilocal households than women with higher parity, though this gradient narrows slightly over time. Similar patterns are observed among women 3-12 months postpartum, with patrilocal residence increasing from 59 percent to 68 percent.

\subsection{Sample means by household structure}
Next we look into the change in trend of household structure with pregnant women’s autonomy and maternal healthcare outcomes. We further look into the economic status of these households using ownership measures. Table \ref{tab:sumstats} presents the summary statistics for autonomy, wealth and maternal healthcare outcomes. 

Among currently pregnant women, there is clear evidence of difference in decision making power. In NFHS 3 (2005-06) 51 percent of pregnant  women in joint households reported having no say in their own healthcare, compared to 32 percent of women in nuclear households. By NFHS-5 (2019-2021), these shares declined to  25 percent to 18 percent, respectively. Although overall autonomy improved over time, the gap between joint and nuclear households persists. A similar pattern is observed for women’s ability to visit family and friends. 

In contrast, joint patrilocal households consistently exhibit higher levels of material resources across all survey rounds, as indicated by asset ownership measures.

Among women who gave birth in the 3-12 months preceding the survey, maternal healthcare utilization is higher in joint households. In NFHS 3, 45 percent of women in joint households delivered in a health facility compared to 34 percent in nuclear households. Institutional delivery increased substantially by NFHS 5 but differences across household structures remained. A similar pattern observed for four or more antenatal care visits. 

Wealth measures in this context also indicate that joint patrilocal households consistently have greater access to material resources across all rounds. 





\subsection{Regression Results}

Figure \ref{fig:regresults} presents estimates of $\beta_1$ from equations \ref{eq:nocontrols} and \ref{eq:controls} for each outcome and survey round, interpreted as the difference in the probability of the outcome between women living in patrilocal and nuclear households.

\subsubsection{ Maternal healthcare use outcomes:}

In models without wealth controls women in patrilocal households exhibit higher levels of maternal healthcare utilization across survey rounds. In NFHS 3 women residing in joint households were approximately 6 percentage points more likely to deliver in a health facility than their counterparts in nuclear households. This advantage persists in NFHS 4 and NFHS 5, though the magnitude declines modestly over time. A similar pattern is observed for having received four or more antenatal visits. In NFHS 3 patrilocal residence is associated with roughly 5 percentage point higher likelihood of receiving adequate antenatal care. The gap remains positive and statistically significant in subsequent rounds.

However, including wealth controls substantially decreases the magnitude differences. Once household assets are accounted for, the patrilocal advantage in institutional delivery and antenatal care is reduced to small and in some cases statistically indistinguishable differences. It is important to note that the difference in maternal healthcare outcomes narrows over time with approaching zero, indicating diminishing gap between patrilocal and nuclear households. 

\subsubsection{Autonomy outcomes}

In contrast, patrilocal residence is consistently and robustly associated with lower levels of decision making power. In NFHS 3 women in joint households are approximately 14 percentage points more likely to report having no say in their own healthcare and nearly 20 percentage points more likely to report no say in visits to family and friends. Although these gaps narrow over time, they remain statistically significant in NFHS 5.

Controlling for wealth does not significantly reduce  autonomy gaps by household type. The difference between patrilocal and nuclear households remains substantial even after adjusting for household assets and amenities. Thus the autonomy disadvantages between the two household residences cannot be explained by economic differences. 


\subsubsection{Trends in autonomy gaps over survey rounds}

Results from the pooled regressions with interactions between household structure and survey round confirmed that autonomy gap has been narrowing over time. Between NFHS 3 and NFHS 4 the gap in having no say in healthcare declined by 6.5 percentage points and the gap in mobility related decision making declined by 11.5 percentage points. Between NFHS 4 and NFHS 5 these gaps continued to shrink though at a smaller magnitude of 2 and 2.5 percentage points respectively. All estimates remain statistically significant even after the inclusion of state fixed effects and wealth controls. 
 

\section{Discussion}

Using nationally representative survey data for India from 2005-2021, we document a moderate reversal in the earlier trend towards nuclear household residence, with pregnant women increasingly residing in patrilocal joint households.  Examining how maternal autonomy and healthcare utilization differs across household structures, we find that pregnant women in patrilocal households report lower decision making authority over their healthcare and mobility yet they exhibit higher utilization of maternal health services including institutional delivery and antenatal care. This apparent healthcare utilization advantage is largely mediated by household economic status, because after controlling for wealth, the difference between patrilocal and nuclear households in these outcomes is either statistically or practically insignificant. On the other hand, the disadvantage in autonomy over own healthcare use or visits to family and friends among patrilocal households persists even after controlling for wealth.  But further analysis indicates that the disadvantage in autonomy has also been decreasing over the survey rounds. Decomposition analysis further indicates that improvements in autonomy and healthcare over time are driven primarily by changes within household structures rather than by shifts in the distribution of women across household types.


These findings fit into the existing literature which supports the role of economic status in mediating patrilocal joint family advantages (\cite{allendorf2013going} \cite{breton2021modernisation}). Rather than reflecting an inherent benefit of joint residence, the maternal healthcare advantage observed here appears closely tied to the greater material resources available in many joint families. \cite{breton2021modernisation} shows that nucleation in India is not a monolithic process and hides multiple mechanisms which fosters as well as curtails nucleation of households. Their findings further show the rise of nuclear households among less educated couples compared to economically stable couples which is consistent with our findings of more material resources among joint patrilocal households. This pattern suggests that changes in household structure in India are not solely driven by cultural or social factors, but are also shaped by persistent socioeconomic inequality. 

It is also important to note here that we observe the gap between the patrilocal household structures and the nuclear household structures has also been reducing over the 15 years raising the question whether the society is becoming more progressive in general. \cite{pesando2019rethinking} in their review of \cite{goode1963world} theories of family patterns pointed out that despite the idea that the rationale of industrialization is best fitted for nuclear families, this was observed to be empirically not true. Further, they also showed extensive evidence of diverging family patterns across socioeconomic groups within the societies.Thus, the shift might be in tandem with the socio-economic and structural transformation.  But at the same time our findings also indicate that 30 percent (ref: appendix table) of the shift to nuclear households can be explained by the composition of women at lower parities. This occurs as women move into their husband’s familial home soon after marriage \cite{breton2021modernisation} and are expected to begin childbearing after co-habitation begins \cite{ibarra2020desire}. Together this suggests that the observed shift towards nuclear households does not simply reflect a general process of social progress but is also shaped by changing parity composition of women across household structures. 

In contrast to the material pathways underlying maternal healthcare use, differences in women’s autonomy indicators between nuclear and patrilocal households reflect enduring hierarchical dynamics within joint families. Historically, newly married in patrilocal households occupied the subordinate position within multigenerational family structures where senior members exercise greater authority. Our findings of persistently lower reported decision making power among women in patrilocal households are consistent with long standing gender and age hierarchy. At the same time, recent research suggests that the quality of relationship between daughter in law and their in-laws can shape women’s lived experiences within joint families, as positive relational ties are associated with greater perceived autonomy in patrilocal settings \cite{char2010influence}. Combined with our evidence that improvements in autonomy over time are not driven by changes in the composition of women across household structures, these findings point towards evolving gender dynamics within both joint and nuclear families and convergence of autonomy outcomes across the two household structures. 

This study has important limitations. Our indicators of maternal healthcare capture utilization but not quality. Reporting four more antenatal visits does not ensure adequacy of care, continuity with the same provider, or receipt of recommended services. Likewise institutional delivery encompasses substantial variation across public and private facilities and does not distinguish differences in clinical quality, mode of delivery, or patient  experience. Measures of autonomy rely on self-reported decision making authority and may not fully capture informal influence or relational dimensions power within households.  Finally, as NFHS data are cross sectional and measured at the time of the survey, they may not capture the fluid and transitional nature of residence arrangements surrounding marriage and childbirth, as women may move between the different households during this period. This can lead to misclassifications of household structure and obscure how residence shapes autonomy and maternal healthcare outcome.

Together these findings indicate that narratives of nuclearization and women’s empowerment are not linear. Further, the resurgence of joint residence does not uniformly disadvantage pregnant women, nor does it independently explain improvements in maternal healthcare. Rather, household structure appears to be closely linked with broader processes of economic stratification and changing intra household dynamics. Autonomy and healthcare utilization operate through different pathways, underscoring the need to examine family forms not as fixed categories but as evolving institutional contexts. Understanding how economic resources and relational dynamics intersect within households remains central to explaining contemporary shifts in women’s well-being.
